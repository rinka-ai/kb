# Database Reactivation

## Summary

Database reactivation is the first commercial lever in the Middleton agency model because it can create revenue from assets the business already owns. The operational version is simple: identify eligible dormant contacts, send a relevant reason to re-engage, route replies into appointment booking, remind people to show, and measure revenue. The hard part is not the chatbot. It is consent, data quality, offer fit, and downstream fulfillment.

For a new agency, reactivation is attractive because it can create proof before paid ads. For the client, it can create working capital and trust before asking them to spend more money on marketing.

## Workflow

1. Export the dormant lead or former-customer list from the CRM, booking system, POS, or ad lead forms.
2. Clean and classify the list: source, age, last interaction, channel, location, service interest, status, suppression flags, and duplicate records.
3. Verify permission for each channel before outreach: SMS, phone, email, voicemail, or messenger each has different consent and opt-out expectations.
4. Segment the campaign by buyer state: old inquiry, no-show, former customer, churned customer, inactive member, lost estimate, or past purchaser.
5. Write a plain-language reactivation reason: anniversary, seasonal opening, unused benefit, new availability, limited pilot, check-in, or relevant offer.
6. Route replies into a booking path with human fallback for pricing, edge cases, complaints, opt-outs, and sensitive requests.
7. Send confirmation and reminder messages after booking so response becomes show rate, not just conversation.
8. Attribute outcomes back to the list source: replies, bookings, shows, sales, revenue, opt-outs, complaints, and refund/churn behavior.

## Message Principles

- Name the existing relationship or context so the recipient knows why they are being contacted.
- Make the first response low-friction: yes/no, choose a time, ask a question, or claim a specific appointment slot.
- Avoid pretending the conversation is purely manual if automation is doing the work.
- Offer a clear opt-out path and honor it across all future campaigns.
- Keep the offer truthful and locally relevant. A generic "AI growth" pitch is weaker than a concrete reason to return.
- Do not over-optimize for replies if the client cannot handle the appointments.

## Metrics

- Eligible contacts after consent and suppression checks
- Delivery rate, bounce rate, opt-out rate, complaint rate
- Response rate and positive response rate
- Booked appointments and cost per booked appointment
- Show rate, sales rate, revenue collected, gross margin
- Revenue per eligible contact and revenue per contacted lead
- Human handoff rate and unresolved conversation rate

## Failure Modes

- Treating "old lead" as permission to text or call. This is the main compliance trap.
- Importing dirty data that creates duplicate outreach, wrong names, or irrelevant offers.
- Optimizing for booked appointments while no-shows, low intent, or poor sales process destroy revenue.
- Running the same message across every niche without matching LTV, seasonality, urgency, and buying trigger.
- Failing to connect bookings and sales back to the original contact list, which leaves the agency with anecdotes instead of evidence.

## Evidence And Authority

- Lead-source authority: [FTC's Follow the Lead staff perspective](https://www.ftc.gov/reports/follow-lead-workshop-staff-perspective) treats lead generation as a data ecosystem with consumer-protection and privacy risks, so a reactivation workflow needs source, consent, transfer, and disclosure records.
- Email authority: [FTC CAN-SPAM guidance](https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/resources/can-spam-act-compliance-guide-business) applies to commercial email, including B2B and former-customer promotional messages, and requires truthful routing, non-deceptive subject lines, sender identification, a physical address, opt-out, and prompt opt-out honoring.
- Phone/voice authority: [FCC TCPA AI voice ruling](https://docs.fcc.gov/public/attachments/FCC-24-17A1.pdf) confirms AI-generated human voices fall under artificial/prerecorded voice restrictions; telemarketing use requires prior express written consent.
- Text/consent authority: the FCC's [TCPA consent FAQ](https://docs.fcc.gov/public/attachments/DOC-408396A1.pdf) reiterates that TCPA applies to voice calls and text messages, that certain autodialed/artificial/prerecorded calls require consent, and that telemarketing to DNC-listed numbers needs seller-specific written permission.
- Evidence status: the Middleton reactivation examples are useful case prompts, but the KB should require its own eligibility audit, message approval, opt-out logs, and revenue attribution before treating results as transferable.

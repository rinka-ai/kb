# UGC Ads

## Summary

UGC ads work when they feel like a real person showing a real problem, moment, demo, or result in the language of the audience. They fail when they become brand scripts wearing casual clothes. In paid growth, UGC is best treated as a creative supply chain with research, briefing, rights, compliance, editing, testing, and refresh loops.

## Types

- Real customer UGC: customers submit or permit use of their own content.
- Creator UGC: paid creators produce content in a native style for brand use.
- Partnership ads: creator content is permissioned and promoted with creator identity attached.
- Actor-led UGC-style ads: performers create native-looking ads without being real customers.
- AI-generated UGC-style assets: synthetic avatars or generated scenes mimic creator formats.

These should not be collapsed into one bucket because disclosure, credibility, cost, and performance risks differ.

## Why UGC Works

UGC can reduce ad resistance because it resembles platform-native content. It can also supply proof that polished brand creative struggles to communicate: how the product looks in use, what a customer noticed first, what problem triggered interest, and what objections were resolved.

The strongest UGC usually starts with:

- a specific role, situation, or pain
- an honest reaction
- a curiosity moment
- a real demo
- a relatable mistake or surprise
- a customer phrase from VoC research

## Script Structure

A useful direct-response structure is:

1. Hook
2. Problem
3. Solution
4. Value proposition
5. Social proof
6. Call to action

The structure is scaffolding. The final read should sound like the creator, not the brand. Billo's hook analysis is especially useful here: high-performing hooks often feel honest, situational, funny, vulnerable, or specific before they feel like ads.

## Creator Brief

A strong UGC brief should include:

- target audience and situation
- product use case
- one primary angle
- required proof or demo
- prohibited claims
- disclosure requirements
- brand language to avoid
- natural speech guidance
- visual requirements and safe zones
- usage rights, whitelisting, and partnership ad permissions
- deliverables by ratio and duration

## Compliance

UGC is not exempt from advertising rules. FTC guidance requires material relationships to be disclosed clearly. Meta policy still applies to creator ads, including personal attributes, restricted categories, deceptive practices, and landing-page review.

Risk signals:

- creator claims a result they did not experience
- disclosure only appears where users are unlikely to notice it
- a testimonial implies typical results without evidence
- health, finance, or beauty copy diagnoses the viewer
- AI or actor content is presented as real customer experience

## Testing

Track UGC performance by more than creator name:

- hook type
- angle
- proof type
- creator persona
- visual setting
- first three seconds
- CTA
- offer
- landing page
- disclosure format

This lets the team learn why a piece worked instead of simply asking the same creator for more of everything.

# Review Referral Automation

## Summary

Review and referral automation exists because local-business demand is trust-mediated. People look at reviews before contacting a provider, and referred leads often arrive warmer than cold paid traffic. The Middleton transcript frames this as a pillar of the acquisition system: ask customers for feedback, route happy customers toward public reviews, and ask satisfied customers for referrals.

The enriched KB version is more cautious. The exact gating patterns in the Middleton and Pavlo transcripts are compliance-sensitive, especially when a funnel is described as preventing bad reviews. A durable agency workflow should ask real customers in a neutral, platform-safe way, separate feedback collection from public-review manipulation, disclose or avoid incentives where required, and keep referral rewards distinct from review requests.

## Jobs To Be Done

- Increase visible trust for future buyers.
- Surface customer feedback that helps the business fix service problems.
- Create referral demand that can reduce blended CAC.
- Improve local discovery only as a secondary effect, because review count and rating are not a controllable ranking formula.
- Give the sales call real proof, not vague "we do AI" language.

## Safer Workflow

1. Define the triggering event: completed appointment, project completion, membership milestone, purchase, or service follow-up.
2. Ask for feedback from real customers in a way that does not screen only likely-positive reviewers.
3. Invite public reviews without conditioning the request on a positive rating, specific content, or incentive.
4. Route negative or operational feedback to staff for service recovery without suppressing legitimate public criticism.
5. Ask for referrals as a separate customer-growth workflow, with clear terms for any raffle, discount, gift card, or reward.
6. Respond to public reviews with short, specific, privacy-safe replies.
7. Track reviews and referrals through the same revenue dashboard as paid leads.

## Copy Boundaries

- "Tell us how we did" is usually safer than "Give us five stars."
- "Here is a link if you would like to share your experience publicly" is safer than only asking customers who first gave a high internal rating.
- Referral incentives should be disclosed, simple, and separate from the review request.
- Do not ask customers to mention specific keywords, services, or claims in a review.
- Do not let staff pressure customers in person, especially in healthcare, financial, legal, housing, or other sensitive contexts.

## Metrics

- Eligible customer count and contact rate
- Feedback response rate
- Public-review request rate and public-review completion rate
- Rating distribution, review velocity, and response rate
- Referral asks, referral leads, booked referrals, referral sales, referral revenue
- Complaint rate, service-recovery cases, and repeat-issue themes

## Risks

- Review gating: asking only happy customers for public reviews or discouraging unhappy customers from posting.
- "No bad reviews" funnels: filtering public review invitations based on private sentiment can create platform and consumer-protection risk.
- Incentive ambiguity: rewards that appear tied to positive public reviews instead of neutral feedback or referrals.
- Privacy leakage in review replies.
- Using fake, staff-written, or non-customer reviews.
- Treating more reviews as proof that the agency caused revenue growth without tracking referral and sales outcomes.

## Evidence And Authority

- Google local-ranking authority: [Google Business Profile local-ranking guidance](https://support.google.com/business/answer/7091/improve-your-local-ranking-on-google) says relevance, distance, and prominence shape local results; review count and positive ratings can contribute to prominence, and replies can help businesses stand out.
- Google review-request authority: [Google's tips to get more reviews](https://support.google.com/business/answer/3474122) allow asking customers through a review link or QR code, but emphasize genuine experiences, no incentives for posting/changing/removing reviews, valuing all reviews, and non-promotional replies.
- Google review-policy authority: [Google prohibited and restricted content](https://support.google.com/business/answer/7400114) does not allow incentives for reviews, discouraging negative reviews, selectively soliciting positive reviews, pressure on premises, or requests for specific review content.
- FTC review authority: [FTC review-solicitation guidance](https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/resources/soliciting-paying-online-reviews-guide-marketers) warns against reviews from non-customers, likely-positive-only solicitation, staff/family reviews without disclosure, and positivity-conditioned incentives.
- FTC rule authority: the FTC's [Consumer Reviews and Testimonials Rule Q&A](https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/resources/consumer-reviews-testimonials-rule-questions-answers) adds that agencies, PR firms, review brokers, and reputation-management companies can be liable for fake reviews, sentiment-conditioned incentives, review suppression, and fake social proof.
- Market evidence: [BrightLocal's Local Consumer Review Survey 2026](https://www.brightlocal.com/research/local-consumer-review-survey/) supports the commercial importance of reviews and review responses, but it is practitioner survey evidence, not a policy source.
- Evidence status: the transcript's raffle and rating-gate sequence should not be copied as-is. The compliant version is neutral feedback, platform-safe review requests, separate referral incentives, and documented disclosures where incentives are used.

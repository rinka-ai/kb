# AI-Native Content Machine

## Summary

Alex Lieberman's content-machine digest is useful because it treats content as an operating system, not a prompt. The creator keeps the first and final mile: choosing the idea, supplying context, reviewing the final draft, and approving publication. The middle is a skillized workflow: mine internal and external signals, research claims, interview for specificity, preserve raw material, draft against style/context, critique through multiple lenses, repurpose natively, and learn from the delta between first draft and final edit.

This fits the KB's broader pattern from agentic engineering and skills sources: AI leverage compounds when repeated work becomes explicit artifacts and procedural modules. For content, the essential artifacts are the idea vault, sourced research report, raw interview/production file, content-type spec, style guide, reviewer feedback, derivative assets, performance data, and creator-specific lessons.

## Durable Pattern

- Start by mapping the existing human process before assigning work to AI.
- Put human taste at the gates where it is hardest to recover from error: premise selection, missing context, brand/persona judgment, and final approval.
- Use AI for the high-throughput middle stages, but force evidence-bearing artifacts rather than generic drafts.
- Preserve exact raw material before refinement so real stories, numbers, emotional anchors, and quotes stay available.
- Separate editorial fixes from information gaps. The machine can rewrite weak prose, but missing stories, facts, or numbers must route back to the creator or research/interview loop.
- Treat repurposing as native creation, not resizing: each platform derivative needs its own hook, format, and review pass.
- Convert final human edits into durable creator memory, then let confirmed lessons override the older style guide when they conflict.

## System Shape

1. Signal capture: internal "spikes" from Slack, Notion, calls, Gmail, and an external reader feed.
2. Idea triage: qualifying ideas move into a vault, but the human selects the premise.
3. Research: a sourced report separates facts from opinions and checks claims adversarially.
4. Interview: AI prompts push for stories, numbers, specificity, and emotional detail.
5. Raw production: transcripts and extracted moments are preserved before polish.
6. Refinement: the draft uses the style guide, content-type spec, past feedback, and raw material.
7. Council review: automated reviewers score the draft and route information gaps back upstream.
8. Revision and repurposing: anchor content and derivatives iterate to the same quality bar.
9. Learning: the system compares the first draft with the human-approved final and updates creator-specific lessons.

## Guardrails

- Treat the commercial pipeline claim as self-reported. The source is useful operating evidence, not independent attribution proof.
- A council of famous reviewer personas can become theater unless it produces concrete, routable findings.
- Score thresholds such as 9/10 are only useful if calibrated against real audience, brand, or pipeline outcomes.
- Employee creator programs need explicit consent, review rights, brand boundaries, and compensation or incentive clarity.
- Learning loops can overfit to one final edit unless lessons are confirmed, scoped, and reversible.
